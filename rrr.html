SmartShopper

Designing a smarter grocery shopping experience — research-led redesign to reduce friction and increase satisfaction.

Project Overview

SmartShopper helps busy shoppers plan and execute grocery trips with confidence. The product goal was to reduce decision friction, speed up shopping trips, and increase satisfaction for frequent grocery users and budget-conscious families.

My Role & Responsibilities

  • Led user research: interviews (n=12), usability tests (n=8) and diary studies (n=10).
  • Synthesized findings into personas, journey maps, and prioritized feature backlog.
  • Designed mid- to high-fidelity prototypes and ran iterative usability tests.
  • Worked closely with frontend engineers to implement changes and measure impact.

Research Process

We began with exploratory research to understand shoppers' pain points: time pressure, unclear store layouts, and difficulty remembering items. Research methods included:

  • Contextual interviews — in-person and remote shopping observations.
  • Diary studies — daily logs for 2 weeks capturing shopping decisions and frustrations.
  • Quant analysis — event funnel review to identify drop-off during checkout and list management.

Key Insights

  1. Shoppers value speed: long lists and unclear prioritization lead to abandoned carts.
  2. Families need flexible lists that can be shared and prioritized across devices.
  3. There is strong desire for shopping guidance (smart suggestions based on recipes and past purchases).

Design Process & Iteration

We ran a design sprint focused on list prioritization and a “quick trip” flow. Iterations included:

  • Low-fidelity sketches to validate flow and priority controls.
  • Mid-fidelity wireframes to test the “quick trip” mode with users.
  • High-fidelity prototypes in Figma for usability sessions (8 participants) and A/B testing live with a small cohort.

Final Solution

The final product included:

  • Quick Trip Mode: prioritizes items for short store visits.
  • Shareable Lists: real-time sync and item assignment for family members.
  • Smart Suggestions: recipe-aware suggestions and substitutes to reduce decision fatigue.

Impact & Reflection

After release, internal metrics tracked a 32% increase in measured satisfaction and a 25% reduction in time-to-complete our key checkout task. More importantly, qualitative feedback highlighted that users felt less rushed and more confident while shopping.

What I learned: prioritize tiny wins early — a focused 'quick trip' flow had outsized impact. Involving engineering early in prototype validation accelerated delivery and improved fidelity of the implemented experience.
